There are cities where Holy Week is a spectacle. In Cádiz it is a conversation between the neighbourhoods and their history, between the living and those who are gone, between a city that looks out to sea and a faith that has been anchored in its stones for centuries.

## A Holy Week with its own personality

Holy Week in Cádiz does not compete with Seville or Málaga. It does not need to. It has its own identity, forged in a peninsular city surrounded by water on almost all sides, where processions move through narrow streets that suddenly open onto the Atlantic horizon.

There is something unique about watching a Christ or a Virgin process alongside the sea. The smell of salt air and incense mingles in a way that happens nowhere else. The Atlantic breeze stirs the pointed hoods of the penitents. The sound of drums bounces off whitewashed facades. Cádiz during Holy Week is a painting in motion.

## The brotherhoods and their floats

Cádiz has twenty-one penitential brotherhoods, each with its own history, neighbourhood and character. Some of the most beloved:

The **Hermandad de la Palma**, from the Santa María neighbourhood, is one of the oldest and most popular. Its palio float, carrying the Virgen de la Palma, moves through the streets of the old town between two walls of devotees watching in silence.

The **Hermandad del Prendimiento** has one of the most impressive mystery floats in the city, with a carving of great expressiveness that seems to stop time as it passes.

The **Hermandad de la Piedad**, with its image of the Virgen de las Angustias, creates one of the most emotional moments of Holy Week in Cádiz when it passes through the Arco de la Rosa on Good Friday afternoon.

## Palm Sunday and Holy Monday: the emotional beginning

Although every day has its charm, Palm Sunday and Holy Monday mark the true emotional start of the week. The streets begin to fill, balconies are decorated, and the atmosphere changes in an almost tangible way. The city moves from ordinary time to sacred time.

Gaditanos living elsewhere return home. Those who live here bring chairs out to their doorways. The bars in the centre fill until late, mixing devotion and conversation in that characteristically gaditano way of understanding life.

## How to experience Holy Week in Cádiz

To make the most of it, a few details are worth knowing:

- **Check the official itinerary**: Each brotherhood has its departure time and route. The Ayuntamiento publishes programmes in advance.
- **Find the floats in the narrow streets**: The impact of a float in a tight alley, with the music and the smell of wax, is incomparable.
- **Go to the Cathedral on Good Friday**: The setting of the New Cathedral, with its golden dome, is one of the most impressive frames for watching the processions.
- **Come early or stay late**: Night-time processions have a completely different atmosphere — more intimate, more intense.

## Beyond faith

Holy Week in Cádiz is also first-rate artistic heritage. The floats hold carvings from the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, works by sculptors of the Andalusian school. The embroidered vestments, the silver candelabras, the palio floats with their gilded poles are processional works of art that spend the rest of the year resting in the churches and chapels of the old town.

Holy Week in Cádiz does not ask for faith. It only asks for attention and respect. The city provides everything else.
