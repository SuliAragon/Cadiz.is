There are products that tell a story before they reach the plate. The almadraba tuna of Barbate does not tell a story: it tells three thousand years of history. The same trap, the same sea, the same patient waiting for the fish to arrive.

## The almadraba: a trap with memory

The almadraba is a fishing system that the Phoenicians brought to these shores three millennia ago. Its name comes from Arabic — Arab fishermen refined it over centuries — and literally means "place where one strikes". That is not a poetic metaphor: when the tuna fall into the death chamber, the work is hard, physical and ancient.

The system consists of a series of nets anchored to the seabed that form a labyrinth of chambers. The tuna, migrating from the Atlantic to the Mediterranean to spawn, follow the coast and fall into the trap. The final chamber — the chamber of death — is where the levantá takes place: the moment when fishermen raise the nets from small boats and the tuna surge to the surface in bloodied water.

It is a brutal and fascinating spectacle at the same time. A ritual repeated every May since time immemorial at the four municipalities with active almadrabas: Conil, Barbate, Zahara de los Atunes and Tarifa.

## Why almadraba tuna is different

The Atlantic bluefin tuna — Thunnus thynnus — that crosses the Strait is the most prized specimen in the world. The Japanese call it "hon-maguro" and pay astronomical prices for it. What makes the almadraba catch particularly sought after is a combination of factors:

The tuna arrive at the Strait laden with fat accumulated during their lives in the North Atlantic. That fat, distributed through the muscles in fine veins, is what gives the flesh its extraordinary texture and deep, marine flavour that is rich without being aggressive.

Almadraba fishing also causes very little stress to the animal. A tuna caught without stress has flesh in better condition than one that has fought for hours on a longline hook. The difference shows on the plate.

## How to eat almadraba tuna in Barbate

Barbate is the epicentre of tuna culture in the province. The town has a historic canning industry — mojama, roe, loins in olive oil — that has exported the product around the world. But the best way to eat it is fresh, during the levantá season, between April and June.

**Ventresca** is the fattiest, softest cut — the crown jewel. On the griddle, with no seasoning beyond salt and a thread of olive oil, it is an experience that redefines what a fish dish can be.

**Tarantelo** is the cut from the anterior ventral area, highly valued for its balance between fat and lean. Over charcoal it is sublime.

**Mojama** is the loin cured in salt and dried in the wind of the Strait. Sliced as thin as paper, with concentrated and intense flavour, served over a ripe tomato or with toasted almonds, it is one of the most authentic appetisers in the Cádiz culinary tradition.

## Season and reservations

The almadraba season lasts only a few weeks, from April to June, depending on the year and the catches. Restaurants in Barbate, Zahara de los Atunes and Conil fill up in those days with enthusiasts arriving from all over Spain.

If you want to witness a levantá in person, some companies organise boat trips to observe the fishing. It is an experience that does not fade.

Almadraba tuna is not just food. It is cultural heritage, it is sustainability — the almadraba is one of the most selective and respectful fisheries in existence — and above all, it is the deepest flavour of the Strait.
