There are cities that have history. Cádiz is history. A history so long it feels almost uncomfortable: three thousand years of uninterrupted human presence on the same strip of land, surrounded by water on almost every side, staring out at the Atlantic.

## Gadir: the Phoenician origin

Around 1100 BC, Phoenician sailors from Tyre — in present-day Lebanon — founded a colony on this small peninsula that they called Gadir, meaning "walled enclosure" or "fenced place" in their language. It was the westernmost known point of the ancient world. Beyond it, according to legend, lay only the chaos of the endless ocean.

The Phoenicians did not come to colonise in any aggressive sense: they came to trade. Cádiz was from the very beginning a mercantile city, a node on the tin, silver and salt routes that connected the Mediterranean with northern Europe and the Atlantic coasts of Africa. That commercial and cosmopolitan vocation has persisted, in various forms, throughout its entire history.

The temple of Hercules-Melkart that the Phoenicians raised at the southern tip of the island of Erytheia was for centuries one of the most famous and visited sanctuaries in the ancient world. Julius Caesar wept before its statue, legend has it, upon contemplating what Alexander the Great had conquered at the same age.

## Romans, Arabs and the Atlantic

Rome came later and transformed Gadir into Gades, one of the most prosperous cities in the Empire. Its inhabitants enjoyed Roman citizenship, an enormous privilege for a provincial city. The Roman city produced olive oil, garum — the fermented fish sauce the Romans put on everything — and traded with half the known world.

With the fall of the Empire and the Visigothic invasions, Cádiz entered a long period of decline. The Arabs called it Qadis and maintained it as a minor naval base and trading point. The Christian Reconquest, in the thirteenth century, returned the city to the Castilian sphere.

But it was the Discovery of America that relaunched Cádiz to the front line of world history. Columbus sailed from Palos in 1492, but his subsequent voyages — and those of all the conquerors and explorers of the sixteenth century — passed through Cádiz. By the eighteenth century, the city was the wealthiest in Spain: seventy-five per cent of trade with the American colonies passed through its port.

## The Cádiz of the Courts

In 1812, with Spain occupied by Napoleonic troops, the Cortes gathered in Cádiz to promulgate the first democratic constitution in Spanish history. La Pepa, as it was popularly known — it was proclaimed on 19 March, the feast of Saint Joseph — enshrined principles such as national sovereignty, separation of powers and individual rights.

That this happened in Cádiz was no accident. The city had a liberal, cosmopolitan and mercantile tradition that set it apart from the rest of Spain. The gaditanos were accustomed to dealing with people from all over the world, to debating ideas in the cafés along the promenade, to questioning established hierarchies.

## How to read history in the streets

Walking through Cádiz is reading layers of superimposed history. The Roman Theatre, discovered almost by accident during construction works in the nineteen-nineties, is one of the best-preserved Roman theatres on the Iberian Peninsula. The Cádiz Museum holds the Phoenician anthropoid sarcophagus — a piece unique in the Western world — and a collection of Phoenician ivories astonishing in their refinement.

The city walls, rebuilt and modified over centuries, still encircle much of the old town. The New Cathedral, Baroque and Neoclassical, took a hundred years to build. The houses with lookout towers — from which merchants once watched for their ships arriving from America — are an architectural peculiarity found in no other Spanish city.

Three thousand years cannot fit into a single visit. But in Cádiz, even an afternoon is enough to sense the weight of all that time.
