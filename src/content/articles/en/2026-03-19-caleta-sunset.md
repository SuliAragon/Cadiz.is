Some cities keep their treasures in museums. Cádiz keeps them out in the open, bathed by the Atlantic and painted every evening with all the colours of the horizon. And no treasure is more accessible or more moving than the sunset ritual at La Caleta.

## A beach with history and soul

La Caleta is not large. It fits in the palm of your hand on a map: a small curve of sand between the castles of Santa Catalina and San Sebastián, open to the west. But in that minimal space fits all the soul of Cádiz.

The Phoenicians anchored their ships here three thousand years ago. The Romans knew these waters. And today, lifelong gaditanos share the shore with tourists from all over the world who come looking for exactly this: the authenticity the city preserves without effort.

## The applause ritual

Every evening, as the sun begins to touch the horizon, something curious happens at La Caleta. People stop talking. Children stop running. Conversations fade away, like candles burning out.

And then, when the last orange flash disappears below the water, applause breaks out.

It is not ironic applause or tourist applause. It is sincere, spontaneous, almost instinctive. The people of Cádiz applaud the sun as if it were the close of a great theatrical performance. Because for them, it is.

## How to experience the La Caleta sunset

To enjoy the sunset at La Caleta like a true gaditano, follow these tips:

- **Arrive early**: Try to be at the beach at least 30 minutes before sunset. The best spots fill up quickly.
- **Bring something to sit on**: A towel, a low chair, or simply the willingness to sit on the sand.
- **Order a beer or a fino**: Many locals celebrate the sunset with a drink in hand. The surrounding bars are well positioned.
- **Put your phone away** (at least for the last five minutes). Living the moment is worth more than any photograph.
- **Applaud**: If the sun sets in all its glory, join in the applause. It is one of the most gaditano moments you can experience.

## La Caleta beyond the sunset

The beach is worth visiting during the day too. Although it is not the largest or most peaceful, it has a special charm. The waters are calm, the sand is golden and the two castles that frame it are accessible.

The Castle of San Sebastián, connected by a causeway to the mainland, offers spectacular views. The Castle of Santa Catalina now houses a cultural space with art exhibitions.

## How to get there

La Caleta is in the La Viña neighbourhood, one of the most authentic in Cádiz. You can walk there from the historic centre in about 15 minutes, following the seafront promenade. Bus L1 also stops nearby.

Once there, time slows down a little. That is also Cádiz.
