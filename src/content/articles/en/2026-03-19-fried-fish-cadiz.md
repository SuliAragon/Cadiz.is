There is a phrase that the people of Cádiz repeat with a hint of defiant pride: "The fried fish of Cádiz has no equal." It is not chauvinism. Technically, they are right.

## The technique that changes everything

The secret of Cádiz-style fried fish lies not in the fish alone — though that matters — but in the technique. Andalusian frying, and the Cádiz style in particular, uses olive oil at very high temperature with an extremely thin layer of flour, almost imperceptible. The result is a crispy coating that seals the fish on the outside while leaving it juicy and tender inside. No grease. No heaviness. No taste of oil.

The temperature of the oil is the secret that no cookbook teaches: it must be very hot so that the fish repels the fat rather than absorbing it. Traditional freidurías in Cádiz work with deep clay vessels of oil where the fish swim briefly before emerging golden and perfect.

The frying flour — called locally "harina de pescado" or special flour — is another key. It is finer than regular flour and adheres to moist fish without needing egg or any other binder. Just fish, flour and hot oil.

## The fish of the Cádiz fry

Not everything goes. The Cádiz fry has its protagonists, chosen for their size, texture and flavour:

**The boquerón** — fresh anchovy — is the king. Small, fresh, gutted, fried in seconds and eaten whole, spine and all. Whoever learns to eat fried anchovies with the spine discovers another dimension of the dish.

**The acedía** is the flatfish that exists only in these waters and which, when fried, has a unique texture, crispy outside and melting inside. It is one of those products that cannot be found outside the Bay of Cádiz.

**Cazón en adobo** — marinated dogfish — is not fried immediately but macerated in a mixture of vinegar, garlic, paprika and cumin before being coated in flour and fried. The result is a complex, aromatic bite with that acidic note that makes it addictive.

**Ortiguillas** — sea anemones — are the most local product and the one that surprises visitors most. When fried, they have a spongy texture and a concentrated sea flavour unlike anything else.

## Where to eat the best fried fish

In Cádiz, the freidurías are temples. Not luxury restaurants or starred establishments: stalls or shops where fish is fried and sold directly, in brown paper or a paper cone, to eat in the street or take away.

**Freiduría Las Flores**, on the Plaza de las Flores, is an institution. A guaranteed queue at weekends, but worth it every time. At **El Faro del Puerto** in El Puerto de Santa María, the fry reaches another level in a reference restaurant. At the beach bars along La Caleta, the traditional fritters are served with a view of the Atlantic.

## The ritual of eating from paper

There is something about eating fried fish from brown paper, standing up, in the street, that concentrates the entire gaditano spirit. Simplicity elevated to gastronomic category. The quality of the product as the only argument. Pleasure without pretension.

The people of Cádiz do not need a tablecloth or protocol to eat well. They need good oil, good fish and free hands.
