There is an image that has gone around the world and yet remains just as striking in person: a village of white houses clinging to rock, streets that climb and descend without apparent logic, and a blue sky that makes the whitewash gleam as if it were its own light. These are the white villages of Cádiz.

## Why they are white

The answer is as practical as it is beautiful. The lime used to paint the facades is not merely aesthetic: it is insulating, antiseptic and repels insects. An ancient technology that works perfectly in the southern climate, where summer heat is serious.

But beyond utility, the white villages have built a collective identity around that colour. Facades are repainted with pride before the local patron saint's festival. Competitions for "the most flower-filled balcony" or "the most beautiful street" are deeply rooted traditions. White is not just a colour: it is a declaration of belonging.

## The essential stops on the route

The White Villages Route runs mainly through the Sierra de Grazalema and the Sierra de Cádiz, with excursions towards the Serranía de Ronda. These are the villages you cannot skip:

**Vejer de la Frontera** is probably the most photogenic of all. Its historic centre, declared a protected historic and artistic ensemble, sits on a hill with views of the Atlantic Ocean. Its labyrinthine streets, medieval arches and blend of Arab and Christian influences make it a destination in its own right. Its gastronomic scene is also exceptional.

**Arcos de la Frontera** rises on a sheer cliff above the Guadalete river. From the viewpoint at the Plaza del Cabildo, the view is vertiginous: the river, fields of sunflowers and olive groves, and a horizon that seems infinite. The church of Santa María, Gothic and Mudéjar at once, dominates the square with an imposing presence.

**Grazalema** gives its name to the natural park that surrounds it and is the village with the highest rainfall in all of Spain. That might not sound appealing, but that humidity has created one of the greenest and most surprising landscapes in the province. The pinsapar — a forest of Spanish firs, trees of Tertiary origin that grow only here and in Morocco — is a unique natural wonder.

**Zahara de la Sierra** is reflected in the reservoir that shares its name and has one of the most recognised silhouettes in Andalusia: the Arab castle at the top, the church bell tower halfway up, and the white houses descending to the water.

## How to do the route

The route can be done by car in two or three leisurely days, staying overnight in one of the villages. Public transport between municipalities is limited, so a private vehicle is the most comfortable option.

Spring is the best time: the fields are green, temperatures are pleasant and the villages are not overrun with tourists. Autumn is also spectacular, with the mountain colours changing week by week.

It is worth booking accommodation in advance during high season, especially in Vejer and Arcos, which are the most in-demand destinations.

## Tourism that respects

The white villages are not a theme park. They are living communities where people work, raise children and grow old. Visiting them with respect — not double-parking, not shouting in the streets at two in the morning, supporting local businesses — is the only way to ensure they remain what they are: authentic places, not stage sets.

And that authenticity is, precisely, their greatest treasure.
